\begin{center}
{\Large{\bf{ABSTRACT}}}
\end{center}


Cancer remains one of the leading causes of morbidity globally, and minimally invasive thermal ablation techniques --- particularly Microwave Ablation (MWA) --- have emerged as promising alternatives to traditional surgery for the treatment of solid tumors. Accurate prediction of the ablation zone dimensions (diameter and length) is critical for effective treatment planning, yet current methods rely on either computationally expensive physics-based simulations or limited manufacturer lookup tables that fail to generalize across different antenna designs and operating conditions.

This project presents an AI/ML-based predictive model for estimating ablation zone dimensions from treatment parameters. A comprehensive dataset of 326 samples was curated from 30+ published research papers (2004--2025), encompassing both experimental (ex vivo/in vivo) and simulation (FEM/computational) data across 15 antenna types. Extensive feature engineering was performed, including energy-based features, log transforms, and square-root transforms grounded in physical principles of heat transfer and thermal diffusion.

Six machine learning regression models --- Ridge Regression, K-Nearest Neighbors, Support Vector Regression, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, and Multi-Layer Perceptron --- were trained and evaluated using 10-fold cross-validation with GridSearchCV for hyperparameter optimization. Random Forest achieved the best performance for effective diameter prediction ($R^2 = 0.695$, MAE $= 6.02$\,mm), while Gradient Boosting performed best for length prediction ($R^2 = 0.512$, MAE $= 10.93$\,mm). Feature importance analysis revealed that energy-related features collectively account for 66.7\% of the prediction power, confirming that total energy delivered is the dominant factor in determining ablation zone size. An interactive prediction system was developed for real-time clinical decision support, enabling treatment planning in milliseconds compared to hours for conventional simulation approaches.
